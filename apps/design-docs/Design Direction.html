§ 01

Core Palette

Paper, ink, and a single archival blue.

Color carries meaning here, never atmosphere. The page does the work; the accent is reserved for the one thing on screen that needs your attention.

The surface is a warm off-white, the same temperature as uncoated book stock — slightly creamed, never blue-shifted. The text is a warm near-black that resolves to true ink-on-paper at typical room light. Together they form the entire visible field in 95% of the application. The remaining 5% is a single deep blue with the chromatic character of iron-gall ink — quiet against paper, unmistakable next to it.

A second hue, an oxide red, is held in reserve for genuine anomalies: a sync failure, an unresolvable rule, an override that contradicts ground truth. It never appears decoratively. If you see red on this screen, something is wrong.

§ 02

Type System

Two faces. No more.

A single editorial serif for everything readable, a single humanist monospace for everything measurable. The discipline of a two-face system is not a constraint — it is the position.

The recommendation

Source Serif 4 (Frank Grießhammer, Adobe, 2023) for all running text, headlines, labels, and chrome. JetBrains Mono for numerics, timestamps, identifiers, rule names, run IDs, and any data the user came to inspect rather than read. Both are free, well-hinted, broadly available, and — critically — built for sustained reading rather than headline grandstanding.

Why this pair

Source Serif 4 has the metrics of a workhorse serif and the optical-size axis of a display face. It sets like a journal at body sizes, like a specimen at display, and never tips into Tiempos's mannered editorial weight or Newsreader's slight warmth-as-marketing. Its italics are real italics, not slanted romans. Its tabular figures land cleanly under JetBrains Mono. It will not date.

JetBrains Mono is the humanist monospace that lost its edges: open apertures, generous counters, ligatures off by default. It reads as data, not as terminal — which is the distinction this product depends on. IBM Plex Mono would also work; it is the second choice if the user prefers a touch more institutional voice.

What I rejected, briefly

Inter — overspecified, the lingua franca of every dashboard the brief asks not to look like. Söhne / GT America — paywalled and corporate-by-association. Tiempos — beautiful but reads as fashion-magazine editorial, not technical reference. iA Writer Mono / Duospace — too writerly, draws attention to itself. System fonts — abdication.

§ 03

Status Glyphs

Same vocabulary. Better handwriting.

Evolve, do not replace. The four glyphs are the product's most distinctive visual signature; lose them and the artifact loses its kinship to its own PDF.

The Unicode characters ● ◐ △ ○ are correct in concept and impossible to control in execution: their stroke weights, optical centers, and x-heights drift across fonts and weights. The web app should use a small custom SVG glyph set built to a single optical grid (16×16, 1.25-unit stroke), with the same visual identity as the PDF's glyphs but with consistent metrics, kerning behavior, and pixel rendering across surfaces.

One addition: a fifth glyph for not applicable / not scheduled, distinct from scheduled but empty. The PDF can use an em-dash; the web app gets a single hairline tick. This is the only expansion to the vocabulary — and it earns its place because the existing four conflated two states that the inspection layer needs to distinguish.

Behaviour

Glyphs inherit currentColor, never set their own. At 14px and above they read crisply with the 1.25-unit stroke; below 14px the stroke widens to 1.5 to preserve weight. They never animate. They never carry tooltips by default — the day-detail view is the tooltip. Hovering a glyph in a month cell shifts only the cell's background to paper-deep; the glyph itself does not change.

§ 04

Density & Rhythm

Generous columns, tight rows.

Whitespace is the chrome. Hierarchy comes from scale and position, never from boxes or shadows. A page should feel composed before it feels populated.

The application sits on an 8-pixel base grid, with type set on a 4-pixel half-grid for fine vertical adjustment. Page gutters are generous — 88px minimum on a desktop viewport, scaling fluidly down to 20px on a phone. Prose holds to a 62-character measure; data tables and month grids take the wider 980px column.

Variation lives in vertical rhythm, not horizontal density. Sections are separated by full whitespace blocks of 96px, subsections by 40px, paragraphs by 18px. Within a table or month grid, rows compress to 10–12px of vertical padding — this is the one place where the design is permitted to be dense, because that is where the user is comparing values, not reading prose.

The discoverability principle

This is an inspection tool. The month grid should reveal twelve days of glyph patterns at a glance — and reveal nothing else until clicked. Hover states should be confident but quiet: a single background tint, no chrome, no badges. The information that explains a day's classification is one click away, not one hover away. Tooltips are forbidden as a primary disclosure mechanism; they are permitted only for terse identifiers (e.g. revealing the full UUID behind a truncated run ID).

Where the density lives

Three places are allowed to be dense, by design: the pipeline run log (a monospace table, 13px, tight line-height); the audit trail (similar treatment); and the habit override grid on the day-detail surface, where the user is doing focused administrative work and benefits from compactness. Everywhere else, restraint.

§ 05

Motion Principles

Three motions. Each one earned.

Your instinct is correct. Motion is rationed, not deployed. The application should feel as immediate as turning a page.

The default behaviour is none. State changes resolve in a single frame. Navigation does not slide. Drawers do not animate. Tabs do not crossfade. The application is rendered, not performed.

The three exceptions

One — disclosure fade (120ms, ease-out, opacity only). When the day-detail panel reveals beneath a month cell, or when a pipeline run log expands its lines, the new content fades in over 120 milliseconds. No height transition, no slide. This is the one place where instantaneous appearance produces a perceptual jolt — the eye loses its anchor — and a brief opacity ramp prevents that without claiming any of the user's attention.

Two — focus underline (80ms, ease-out, width only). When a keyboard-focused control gains its focus indicator, the underline grows from left to right over 80 milliseconds. This is the only "interaction" animation in the application and it serves accessibility, not delight.

Three — pulse on "now" (2.4s, infinite, opacity 0.6→1). The single archival-blue dot indicating today in the month grid breathes very slowly. It is the only ambient animation permitted and it exists because today is the one piece of state the user re-orients to on every visit. Even this is debatable; if it reads as decorative in practice, remove it.

Reduced motion

All three exceptions resolve to instantaneous under prefers-reduced-motion: reduce. The pulse stops at full opacity. The fade becomes a swap. The underline appears, not grows. The application looks identical at rest — the only difference is the time it takes to get there.

§ 06

Dark Mode

Don't.

A position, openly argued. I'd rather defend a single refined light theme than ship a competent paper-dark mode that quietly betrays the metaphor.

The artifact you've already built — the PDF on the reMarkable — has exactly one register: paper. The web app is its workshop. A workshop and the page it produces should look like they come from the same shelf. Introducing a dark mode is, materially, a second register for one product. It is two visual systems to maintain and reason about, and the only thing it buys you is the ability to administer your habit system at 2 a.m. in a dark room — which the brief specifically says is not the use case.

The honest tells that you'd be making a dark mode out of completeness rather than need:

  • You don't have a second persona who works at night.
  • The reMarkable is your dark-room device, not this surface.
  • The single accent color you're using carries meaning. It cannot mean the same thing on a #0A0A0A surface as on a #F8F6F1 surface — you'd be designing the system twice.
  • A paper-dark theme done well is a separate aesthetic project. Done poorly it becomes Material Dark with a warm tint.

Recommendation: ship light-only. Bake it in. Don't gesture at prefers-color-scheme. Don't hedge with a toggle. The aesthetic register you've described — editorial, instrumental, restrained — is fully expressible on one surface. The discipline of refusing the second theme is itself a design decision, and a defensible one for a single-user application.

If you later decide you want a night register for late-evening review, treat it as a separate display mode with its own visual language — not "the same UI in darker colors." That is the only version of dark mode worth building, and it does not need to ship in v1.

The honest counterargument

The strongest case for dark is: the inspection tool sometimes runs next to a terminal, and a bright surface feels jarring beside a dark IDE. If you find this happens in practice, build a true paper-dark — surfaces in oklch(20% 0.008 80), text in oklch(86% 0.006 80), accent shifted to a warmer iron-gall oklch(70% 0.07 248) for legibility. But build it as a separate, complete system; do not invert variables.

Avoid — specifically, not generically

  1. Linear-style "minimal" dashboardsMonochrome geometric SVG illustrations, single-pixel borders on cards, a CTA in a gradient pill. The brief says no, but it bears repeating because the gravity is strong.
  2. Notion's emoji-anchored sectionsA 🌱 next to every heading reads as cheerful. Cheerful is not what this product is.
  3. Glassmorphism, frosted blur, "depth"This page is paper. Paper does not have a backdrop-filter.
  4. Big hero numbers in gradient fillsQuantified-self trope. Numbers read as instruments, not as scores.
  5. Lucide / Heroicons at 16 pxA pictogram for "settings" and another for "logs" is not a design system. The four glyphs are the vocabulary; everything else should be a word.
  6. Cards-on-cards, shadow-on-shadowHairlines and whitespace do the structural work. A card is a confession that hierarchy failed.
  7. Apple's "rounded corner everything"Soft radius on a serif page reads as cosmetic. Square corners, full stop. (Maybe 2px on chips. No more.)
  8. Productivity "streaks" UICalendar heat maps, flame icons, "you're on a 14-day streak." Anti-thetical to the calm-over-gamified position.
  9. Tooltip-driven discoveryIf a thing requires a hover to be understood, design it differently. Drawers and detail views, not floating bubbles.
  10. Dark-by-default with violet/teal accentsEvery quantified-self product on Product Hunt has shipped this. It is the visual equivalent of a fitness influencer.
  11. "Figma Community" template energyAuto-layout symmetry with no editorial decisions inside it. A grid is not a design.
  12. Skeuomorphic "journal" texturesThe opposite failure mode. No paper grain, no leather, no fountain-pen brushes. The metaphor is structural, not literal.
